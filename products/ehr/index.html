Clinicians finish sessions and start charting. Notes get written twice — once for the clinical record, once again in the format the payer wants. Clinical documentation

Residential clinicians document the same clinical picture three times a week — once in the session note, again in the weekly summary, and a third time in the utilization review. Then a payer changes its preferred note format and the whole pattern rebuilds itself. BlueStep's clinical record is built to capture the picture once and reuse it everywhere it's needed.

  • Session notes sized for residential: Individual, Family, and Group — each with fields that match how the session actually runs
  • Psychiatrist notes with medication history, mental status, and plan tied directly to the treatment plan
  • Medical and nursing notes that sit next to clinical notes in the same record — no parallel chart
  • DAP or SOAP format, chosen per note type and switchable per payer contract
  • Templates shaped for adolescent residential care — not repurposed from outpatient private practice

BlueIQ reads from the clinical record and drafts the utilization review narrative automatically — your team edits and submits instead of writing from scratch. It's the EHR paired with BlueStep's AI framework, not a separate tool your clinicians have to learn.

Individual session notes

Therapist-led one-on-one sessions documented in a DAP or SOAP template sized for a 50-minute residential hour — subjective, clinical observations, plan, and next objective.

Family session notes

Family therapy captured with participants, session focus, and family-system observations — kept with the patient chart so the clinical team sees the full picture.

Group session notes

Group therapy documented once per patient, with shared group content and a patient-specific clinical impression — no duplicate typing across the roster.

Psychiatrist notes

Psychiatric evaluation, medication review, and mental status captured with direct links to active orders and the treatment plan.

Medical and nursing notes

Medical assessments, nursing shift notes, and incident documentation in the same record — clinical and medical teams work from one chart, not two.

Treatment plans

Ninety-day review cycles arrive whether or not the plan still fits. Templates designed for outpatient work don't map to adolescent residential care, so clinicians rewrite the structure every cycle and the measurable goals drift. BlueStep's builder holds the structure so your clinical team can focus on the clinical picture.

  • Problem > Goal > Objective > Intervention hierarchy — every level is traceable back to a diagnosis
  • Built-in residential library of problems, goals, and interventions for adolescent behavioral health
  • Custom templates your program owns — clone, edit, and version them without waiting on a vendor
  • Ninety-day review cycle built in: prior plan carries forward, prompts show what needs an update
  • Measurable objectives with time frames and evidence fields — not free-text promises

Problem

The diagnosed condition you're treating — pulled from the patient's diagnostic workup, not retyped. Problems tie back to DSM/ICD diagnoses so the clinical picture stays consistent from intake through discharge.

Goal

The measurable clinical outcome tied to the problem — what improvement you and the patient are working toward. Written once, reviewed every ninety days, revised when the clinical picture changes.

Objective

The concrete, time-bound behavior that demonstrates progress on the goal. Objectives carry target dates, evidence fields, and status — so a reviewer can see where the patient actually is, not where the plan claimed they would be.

Intervention

The specific clinical action — chosen from a built-in residential library or a custom template your program owns. Interventions link to the clinician responsible and the frequency required, so nothing falls through between shifts.

eMAR

Paper MAR sheets get lost between shifts. Handwriting gets misread. A missed dose doesn't surface until the next med pass, and the reason gets written on a sticky note that never makes it into the chart. The eMAR generates the daily medication schedule from active orders and keeps the record tight through every shift change.

  • Daily medication schedule auto-generated from active orders — no retyping, no transcription errors
  • Simple checkoff workflow sized for a residential med pass — tap, time-stamp, initial
  • PRN tracking with reason, effect, and follow-up — the clinical story stays with the dose
  • Missed-dose exception flow that captures reason and routes to the nurse supervisor
  • Shift-change handoff view — the oncoming nurse sees what was given, what was missed, and what is due

Lock, unlock, and cosignature

Supervisor signoff is a bottleneck when the rules are hard-coded. Cosignature requirements vary by clinician licensure, by state, and by the payer contract in force that week. Audit surveys want to see every unlock, every override, and every cosignature chain — time-stamped, reason-captured, and visible. BlueStep captures all of it automatically.

  • Notes lock on submission — the clinical record freezes the moment the clinician signs
  • Admin unlock with captured reason and full audit trail — corrections are visible, not hidden
  • Dynamic cosignature chains driven by licensure and program rules — not hard-coded to a single workflow
  • Licensure-aware routing: the system knows a provisional-licensed therapist needs an LCSW cosign and routes accordingly
  • Time-stamped audit log of every lock, unlock, override, and cosign — the history surveyors ask for

Lock on submit, unlock on demand

Notes lock after submission. Admins can unlock for correction with a reason captured in the audit trail — the record shows what changed, who changed it, and why.

Admin override with audit trail

Every admin unlock and override is time-stamped, reason-required, and visible in the record's history. Surveyors see the full chain without you assembling it manually.

Dynamic multi-cosignature

Cosignature chains are driven by licensure and program rules — not hard-coded. The system routes the note to whoever the current rule requires, not whoever the template was built for.

Form and feature-level permissions

Role-based systems break down inside residential. A nurse documents medication passes but edits nothing in the therapy notes. A therapist has editor rights on their own clinical notes but author-only on a peer's. An admissions coordinator reads the clinical record but writes nothing to it. Picking a role from a list can't capture that — so BlueStep doesn't try. Permissions are granted per user, per form, per feature.

  • Per-user permissions — not role-based buckets that force compromises
  • Four access levels per scope: reader, author, editor, no access
  • Per-form granularity — the same user can be an editor on clinical notes and a reader on billing
  • Per-feature granularity — a clinician can author treatment plans without touching lock and unlock controls
  • Inheritance where it helps (e.g., program defaults for new hires) — explicit override where it matters
This is not role-based. You don't pick 'Nurse' or 'Therapist' from a list. You grant each person reader, author, editor, or no access on each form and each feature — per user, per scope. Real residential teams don't fit role buckets, and the permission model reflects that.
